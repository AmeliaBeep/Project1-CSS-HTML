Welcome to Safeguarding Hub

Everyone deserves to feel safe, respected, and protected. Our role is to make safeguarding clear, accessible, and supportive for everyone.

On this site, you'll find straightforward information about what safeguarding means, how to recognise the signs when something isn't right, and what to do if you're worried about someone. What is Safeguarding?

Safeguarding means protecting people's health, wellbeing, and human rights. It is about keeping individuals safe from harm, abuse, neglect, and exploitation — and creating safe environments where everyone can live, learn, and thrive.

Safeguarding is especially important for children, young people, and adults who may be more vulnerable due to age, disability, illness, or life circumstances. But safeguarding is everyone's responsibility.

In practice, safeguarding means:

  • Recognising the signs of abuse or neglect
  • Responding in the right way when concerns arise
  • Reporting worries to the right people or services
  • Promoting environments where people feel safe, respected, and supported

Signs to Watch For

Abuse and neglect are not always easy to see. Knowing the signs can help you recognise when someone may need help and protection.

Possible signs in children and young people:

  • Unexplained injuries or frequent accidents
  • Sudden changes in behaviour, such as becoming withdrawn or aggressive
  • Fear of going home or reluctance to be around certain people
  • Poor hygiene, unsuitable clothing, or consistently being hungry
  • Struggles at school, frequent absences, or loss of concentration

Possible signs in adults at risk:

  • Unexplained injuries or frequent hospital visits
  • Signs of fear, anxiety, or confusion
  • Poor living conditions or lack of basic care
  • Sudden withdrawal from friends, activities, or community
  • Being controlled by others, for example, in finances or decision-making

A single sign on its own may not mean someone is being abused or neglected. What matters most is noticing when there is a pattern of signs or when your instinct tells you something isn't right.

If you are worried, it is always better to speak up and seek advice.

Action Steps

Recognising a safeguarding concern can feel worrying — but taking the right steps quickly can make a real difference. You do not need to have proof of harm. If something feels wrong, it is important to act.

  1. Stay Calm and Listen

    If someone discloses a concern to you, listen carefully. Reassure them that they did the right thing by speaking up. Avoid making promises you can't keep, such as keeping it secret.

  2. Respond Appropriately

    Make sure the person is safe in the moment. Do not confront the person you suspect may be causing harm. Write down what you saw, heard, or were told — using the person's own words if possible.

  3. Report Your Concern

    Contact your organisation's safeguarding lead if you have one. If you believe someone is in immediate danger, call 999 right away. For non-emergencies, contact your local safeguarding board, social services, or the police on 101.

  4. Remember You Are Not Alone

    Safeguarding is everyone's responsibility, but you are not expected to handle it by yourself. By reporting your concerns, you are helping professionals step in to provide the right protection and support.
